Research Neuroplasticity

Discuss how understanding the principles of neuroplasticity benefits people.

Understanding what neuroplasticity provides benefits to people via altering an awareness of how their brains operate. The idea is that your brain is able to be 'shaped' and/or 're-shaped' meaning that no matter where you are in life your current set of skills/knowledge is not set in stone but rather clay...it's only set for now - just waiting for a bit of water and some effort and you're ready to re-shape in new ways.

Discuss how you might engage with the principles of neuroplasticity for your benefit.

I find practice to be a powerful tool, I also like the idea of taking it one step further with a 'use it and improve it' approach meaning to apply these principles to things that I want to focus on rather than things that don't necessarily need to be mastered.

What are some of the ways to increase your neuroplasticity?

  • Video games! (It feels like I've lived my whole life for this moment!)
  • Learn a new language.
  • Make music.

Ultimately, I think as long as you are working towards a learning orientated goal you're on the right track

Research Growth Mindset

Discuss what it is and why it is relevant.

It's the belief that a person's skills or abilities can grow over time. It is relevant because it effectively replaces the idea of failure with the idea of a learning opportunity.

In this exploration, did anything surprise you? Change for you?

Not particularly I have touched base on this stuff before in some management courses I have done.

How will you integrate growth mindset into your learning journey?

Whenever I have attempted to do something there are 4 outcomes:

  • Don't try and succeed = feels great!
  • Don't try and don't succeed = impartial
  • Try and succeed = feels good
  • Try and don't succeed = sucks

Problem is that last one doesn't feel too great, so adopting a growth mindset here is helpful (provided you're open to feedback) as it reframes the shortfall as an opportunity to discover where I went wrong so I can apply the learnings to the next attempt.
