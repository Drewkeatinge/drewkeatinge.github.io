What is emotional intelligence?

The definition of emotional intelligence from dictionary.com is as follows.

nounPsychology.
skill in perceiving, understanding, and managing emotions and feelings.
Abbreviation: EI

There are 4 key building blocks of emotional intelligence.

1. Self-Awareness
2. Self-regulation
3. Motivation
4. Empathy

For someone to possess a high emotional intelligence, all of the above works together. Emotional intelligence is a key leadership quality as it helps them to mentor others and resolve conflict effectively.

How is EQ different to IQ?

EQ is different to IQ in that EQ refers to one's emotional intelligence such as the ability to be self-aware of your own emotions or be aware of what emotions others may be feeling. IQ refers to cognitive abilities such as technical skills, analytical thinking or knowledge retention.

Why is EQ important?

EQ is important as it helps you to get a long with others, which is kinda what civilization is built on huh!

In a workplace environment, if you are in a leadership role it is important to work on your EQ skills as it will help you become a more effective leader and have your staff be much happier in the workplace.

I have experienced both leaders with great EQ skills and leaders that have virtually none.

Leader 1 - great EQ skills

This particular leader wasn't always around all of the time, but that didn't matter because it felt like he was because he was there when it mattered. He knew every staff member by name and would make a point of saying hello to you every day. If you had a problem, you felt like you could go to him and not feel like you were annoying or anything. He would also take a chance on you, meaning that if there was an aspect of the business that you were interested in, he would actually make a plan to help you learn the skills for the role. He had an approach that even if you learned the skills and decided to leave, then it was a feather in his cap as he was helping to put more talented people out into the world. Obviously, like anyone he had his flaws, for example he probably gave people a few more chances than they deserved but overall he was fantastic to work for, so much so that when he moved on to a new business on the other side of the country, multiple people were happy to pack up and move with him to continue working for him.

Leader 2 - no EQ skills

This guy was horrendous to work for. He was there nearly every minute of every day and rather than add value by being there he would subtract value with his presence. He was hugely unapproachable if you had a problem in the workplace and if you did need to talk you needed to book in an appointment outside of your rostered hours because 'he was paying you to do a job not to complain'. After a while I found myself having no respect for him as a leader (which interestingly gave me a surprising amount of confidence when needing to deal with him). This business had a huge staff turnover rate because no one wanted to work there.

The contrast between these two 'bosses' is almost black and white. I can tell you with absolute certainty how important having a leader with developed EQ skills is.
